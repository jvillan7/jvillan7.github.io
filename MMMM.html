      Morgs Mimics and Mansions Munchables (MMMM) is a game where you play as Morg, a mimic who wants to overthrow the current Mimic Monarch to seize the throne! To do so, you must amass a large enough base of followers and prove to them that you can provide a steady source of food, humans.

      Mimics


      Task Distribution

      When we first started making MMMM, I took initiative in becoming the lead programmer in designing each of the various Mimics to be implemented in the game. At the time, our design team was stil determining the types of mimics they wanted to see in the game. So, I directed a small sub team of programmers to make a generic, polymorphic mimic interface to be used by all other mimic classes.

      Here was my inital plan for the overall workflow of our project. Because I was the only one on my team with experience in Unity and C#, this was my inital idea of where my team members could start doing research.

      Unfortunately my teammates eventually became unavailable due to their external commitments and stopped contributing before they could start. Thus, I shouldered the workload on my own.


      Programming Generic Mimics

      The difficulty in programming a generic mimic interface was that could accomodate for whatever Design Team would throw at me. I knew that Design Team would most likely design mimics that change and alter how mimics behave, so I wanted to make sure we first had a baseline of what mimics should do.


      After talking with Design Team, we determined mimics must:
      - Look for humans to eat
      - Attempt to eat nearby humans
      - Take some amount of time to eat humans
      - Take some amount of time digesting its food before it can look for humans to eat again


      With all of this in mind, I created different mimic 'states' utilizing a combination of enums and booleans to determine what a the current state a mimic was in. Each type of mimic would likely have their own unique eating and digesting times. So, I made sure to make those variables customizable for when we make unique mimics. I also included an attack range for mimics because that could easily change from unique mimic to mimic.

      All that was left was to wait and see what unique mimics Design Team would come up with so I could program them.






      Starting with the Skitter Mimic

      The first mimic I was tasked to program was the Skitter Mimic. This mimic would be able to crawl across the floor to hunt for its prey.

      The difficulty in creating this mimic was not getting mimic to move, but rather getting it to stop. Whenever a human would get in range of the Skitter Mimc, the mimic should run up to the human, eat, then return to its starting position to digest its food and to look for more humans to eat.

      There was one significant bugs in programming the Skitter Mimic. When the Skitter would run to eat a person, it would instead look for more humans to eat from its current position rather than from it's starting position. While this was a rare bug to encounter, it was important to solve nonetheless.

      After some trial and error, I solved the bug by significantly increasing the checks to the mimic's current state and the mimic's current position.




      Mapping out the Sleepy Mimic

      The Sleepy Mimic is the only mimic I did not plan to have any direct involvement with. One of my original team members was believed to come back to work on the project. So to make up for lost time, I created a diagram outlining the different states that the Sleepy Mimic would go through in a typical game loop.

      To make sure I didn't lose any time on working on the Skitter Mimic, I made Sleepy Mimic diagram concurrently with my work on the Skitter Mimic.

      Unfortunately my teammate back ended up not being able to contribute to the project due to external commitments.

      Luckily, the Sleepy Mimic was primarily about manipulating the different states of the original mimic interface I made at the start of development. This meant that all that needed to be done was manipulate some of the values and include a new state called MimicState.Sleeping to determine whether or not the Sleepy Mimic was sleeping.






      Animating the Fungal Mimc

      Lastly, by the time I could get to programming the Fungal Mimic, it was already finished by GADIG's Programming Director. So at this point, I was tasked with implementing the Fungal Mimic's animations via Unity's Animator.

      This was a simple task with my experiences working on Necronomicon For this project, I used the sprite sheets for the Fungal Mimic provided by Art Team, put them into Unity's Animation, then used Unity's Animator to create the different animation states. After that all I had to do was incorporate the animation states into the Fungal Mimic's code.




      The Title Screen

      When talking with Design Team about the main menu's screens after recieveing Title Screen art for the game, I believed that there was room for improvement. While I personally did not work on programming the various menus screens, I chose to provide feedback from a User Experience standpoint.

      When designing a main menu I believe that accessibility is of the utmost importance. For MMMM, this meant granting the Play Button the highest salience by giving it larger than the rest of the buttons and centering it.

      When someone looks at an image for the first time they typically start looking at it from the center. In the main menu, this means looking at the Skitter Mimic. The gray slits on top of its eyes help guide the player's down directly to the Play Button - the most important button to access when opening up any game.



      From there, the players eyes will likely go to either the How To Play Button or the Options Button - buttons that have a slightly lower priority than the Play Button. These two buttons still maintain a relatively high priority because they are the two buttons that newer players might be interested in opening instead of the Play Button.

      The Exit Button and the Credit Buttons deserve the lowest priority because they are buttons that would typically only be pressed once during the typical user's play session. While arguments can be made for the Exit Button having a higher priority than other buttons due to it being something that should be pressed every play session, I think putting it in the upper left hand corner was the right idea. For the bottom three buttons, I chose to maintain the ideas of Memory Chunking and the Law of Proximity and limited the bottom part of the screen to only 3 buttons with decent spacing instead of upwards of 5 clustered buttons.
